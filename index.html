Compact Defensive
Turret System

SPONSOR — ING ROBOTIC AVIATION INC.

FIG. A Compact defensive turret integrated into the host airframe — isometric.

Abstract

The purpose of this project is to present the design solution proposed for a compact defensive turret system for ING Robotic Aviation Inc. capable of neutralizing drone attacks while respecting size, mass, and power limitations.

The system incorporates a 12-gauge shotgun with a 50-round magazine into a two-axis pan and tilt which is able of a continuous 360° azimuth rotation and 90° elevation. The design also includes a recoil management mechanism, a surveillance system to be able to operate the system in both day and night conditions, and a sealed structure intended for all-weather service.

To improve the overall performance of the system, several critical components were analyzed and optimized, including the tilt shaft, tilt arm, pan shaft, mounting plate, carriage plate damper interface, and guide rods. These elements were evaluated with respect to structural strength, recoil response, mass, and aerodynamic drag to be able to satisfy the project criteria while maintaining reliability and rigidity.

For the components, the most appropriate model was selected based on their unique rated capacity. The structural optimizations are automatically linked to a CAD model to instantly produce a visual representation of the optimal design.

Poodini — One-Handed
Pet-Waste System

GNG 2101 · TEAM Z21 · CLIENT: QUADRIPLEGIC WHEELCHAIR USER

FIG. A Use-case render — one-handed operation from a seated position, in the field.

Client + brief

Our client is a quadriplegic wheelchair user with limited dexterity, primarily operating with their left hand. They wanted to walk their dog independently — which meant scooping pet waste from a seated position, one-handed, without touching a bag or fumbling controls. Two-handed market scoopers were not an option. Long-handled scoopers existed, but none addressed one-handed use, armrest access, low grip strength, and tool-free disposal in a single device.

Design decisions

  • One-handed only — large rocker switch triggers a servo-actuated claw, sized for very low grip force.
  • Tool-free bag disposal — magnetic attachment, no clips, no twisting.
  • Terrain-adaptive claw — geometry tested across grass, gravel, and uneven surfaces; iterated on direct user feedback.
  • Wrist strap + carbon-fiber shaft — prevents drops, keeps total mass below 500 g while reaching armrest-accessible length.
  • Caregiver-friendly disposal — hygienic, odourless, no disassembly for storage.

Validation

7+ structured client-testing sessions across grip force, reach range, bag-removal time, and terrain adaptability. Claw geometry and wrist-strap sizing iterated on each session's findings. Bench-tested the carbon-fiber rod in bending, and failure-tested the claw in torsion and compression to validate the operational envelope. Won the Accessible Design category at uOttawa Design Day 2025.

Case study · 05 MCG 3307-A · Winter 2025

Balancing an Inverted
Pendulum, Properly

CONTROL SYSTEMS · UOTTAWA · 6-PERSON TEAM

FIG. A Physical apparatus — pendulum upright above a belt-driven carriage on a linear rail.

Plant

A translational cart riding on a rotary-belt-driven linear rail keeps an inverted pendulum upright against physical disturbances. A DC motor drives the belt; an absolute rotary encoder reads the pendulum angle; an Arduino R3 closes the loop. On paper, the kind of project that looks straightforward — and isn't.

Tuning process

We started with the textbook sequence — tune K_P until the pendulum holds for a beat, introduce K_I to kill steady-state error, then K_D for transient response. The textbook sequence didn't quite work until we rewrote the Arduino code from scratch, stripping out a bloated function stack. Suddenly the same gains behaved very differently. From there we settled on a PD-dominant law with a slight loop delay — counterintuitive, but allowed for the oscillatory behavior the system needed before correcting.

What worked, what didn't

  • Live response plot — Arduino streamed angle and PWM into a MATLAB live plot. Once tuning got close, the plot was less useful than just watching the cart; before that, it was the only way to tell which gain was misbehaving.
  • Anti-windup — we added it on a hunch about saturated K_I. It wasn't the problem; we removed it.
  • Pendulum shortening + tip mass — improved stability margin; trial-and-error mass placement ended on a heavy shaft-coupler concentrated-mass at the tip.
  • Rail-end saturation — the inner loop only knows about angle, so the cart can drift to a rail end and get stuck there even with the pendulum balanced. The right fix is a slower outer-loop biasing the inner controller back toward the rail mid-point.

Takeaway

Case study · 06 MCG 3131 · Summer 2025

3-Stage Lateral-Offset
Reversing Gear Reducer

MACHINE DESIGN · GROUP 6 · UOTTAWA

FIG. A Full assembly — three reduction stages, four shafts, gears + pinions, supported by SKF rolling-element bearings.

Spec

Reduce 4 HP / 1200 RPM input to 150 RPM reversed output across an 8:1 overall ratio, with input and output shafts laterally offset. A single-stage reduction was unrealistic at this ratio without one gear being disproportionately large, so we ran the analysis on a three-stage reduction — odd number of stages ensures the required reversed-output direction.

Gear selection

  • Spur gears chosen over helical via decision matrix — primarily to avoid axial thrust forces complicating bearing selection downstream.
  • Targeted ~½ reduction per stage (0.5³ = 1/8) to keep all stages similar in size and noise behavior.
  • Odd tooth counts on all pinions — increases gear life by reducing the number of times the same two teeth mesh (e.g. N₁ = 15, N₂ = 30).
  • Boston Gear catalogue used for all gear sourcing; SKF for bearings.

My contribution — bearing subsystem

I owned the bearing subsystem end-to-end: calculated radial loads, evaluated dynamic load ratings, selected six rolling-element bearings, and verified service life against the ISO 281 / ABMA model. Justified grease lubrication for the start-stop duty cycle and produced bearing implementation specifications including fit, mounting, and contamination control.

Case study · 07 MCG 4144 · 2025–26

Composite Frame for
a Stroller

COMPOSITES DESIGN · TEAM UG20 · UOTTAWA

Brief

MCG 4144 Composites — Team UG20's project was to redesign the structural frame of a Bugaboo Butterfly 2 stroller as a composite laminate. The stroller's existing aluminum frame met the load case but with a mass penalty; our brief was to evaluate whether a CFRP layup could meet the same structural requirements at lower mass while surviving the same in-service loading.

Approach

  • Geometry — reduced the stroller to a parametric frame with measured envelope dimensions (0.50 m × 0.45 m × 0.55 m + 0.15 m + 0.30 m); preserved load-path nodes and joint locations.
  • Load cases — push force, stair-impact reaction, baby + stroller weight (F_R = F_I + W_s + W_b), and ground reaction (F_GRF).
  • Laminate design — selected stack-up to meet the dominant bending and shear cases; iterated ply orientation across six structured assignments.
  • Property prediction — predicted laminate stiffness and failure stresses against the loaded cases; validated against the constraints set in the project brief.

Outcome

A CFRP frame design with documented stack-up, predicted strength, and load-case analysis spanning six structured assignments through to the Final Report. Captured the full design workflow from reverse-engineered geometry through to laminate property prediction.
